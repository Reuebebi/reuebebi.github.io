2. Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation

In 1687, Sir Isaac Newton published his law describing the gravitational attraction between any two objects with mass. This law was revolutionary because it applied universally, from small objects on Earth to vast celestial bodies.

2.1. The Formula for Universal Gravitation

Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation states that every particle in the universe attracts every other particle with a force that is directly proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between their centers.

$$F = G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2}$$

Where:

  • $F$ is the magnitude of the gravitational force between the two masses (in Newtons, N).
  • $G$ is the Universal Gravitational Constant, a fundamental constant of nature, approximately $6.674 \times 10^{-11} \text{ NÂ·m}^2/\text{kg}^2$. This value was first accurately measured by Henry Cavendish.
  • $m_1$ and $m_2$ are the masses of the two interacting objects (in kilograms, kg).
  • $r$ is the distance between the centers of the two masses (in meters, m). It is crucial to measure from center to center, especially for large spherical objects like planets.

The direction of the gravitational force is always attractive, pulling the two masses directly towards each other along the line connecting their centers. This is a crucial distinction from electrostatic forces, which can be both attractive and repulsive.

2.2. The Inverse Square Law

The dependence on $1/r^2$ is known as an inverse square law. This means that if you double the distance between two masses, the gravitational force between them decreases by a factor of $2^2 = 4$. If you triple the distance, the force becomes nine times weaker ($3^2=9$). This inverse square relationship is common in nature for forces that spread out uniformly in three dimensions, such as gravity and electric forces.

2.3. Gravitational Force vs. Other Forces

Gravitational force is often described as the weakest of the four fundamental forces. For example, the electromagnetic force between two electrons is vastly stronger than their gravitational attraction. However, because mass is always positive and gravity is always attractive, its effects accumulate over large distances and for massive objects, making it the dominant force on astronomical scales.

Example: Gravitational Force between Two People

Calculate the gravitational force between two people, each with a mass of $70 \text{ kg}$, standing $1 \text{ m}$ apart.

Given: $m_1 = 70 \text{ kg}$, $m_2 = 70 \text{ kg}$, $r = 1 \text{ m}$, $G = 6.674 \times 10^{-11} \text{ NÂ·m}^2/\text{kg}^2$.

Using $F = G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2}$: $$F = (6.674 \times 10^{-11}) \frac{(70)(70)}{(1)^2}$$ $$F = (6.674 \times 10^{-11}) \frac{4900}{1}$$ $$F = 3.27 \times 10^{-7} \text{ N}$$ The force is approximately $3.27 \times 10^{-7} \text{ N}$. This is an extremely small force, highlighting why we don't feel the gravitational pull of nearby objects in everyday life â€“ it's completely dwarfed by Earth's gravity.

3. Gravitational Fields

Just as we described electric forces using electric fields, we can conceptualize gravitational forces using the idea of a gravitational field. This field describes the influence that a mass exerts on the space around it.

3.1. Definition of Gravitational Field Strength ($\vec{g}$)

A gravitational field strength ($\vec{g}$) at a point in space is defined as the gravitational force per unit mass that would be exerted on a small test mass placed at that point. It is a vector quantity.

$$\vec{g} = \frac{\vec{F}_g}{m_0}$$

Where:

  • $\vec{g}$ is the gravitational field strength (in Newtons per kilogram, N/kg).
  • $\vec{F}_g$ is the gravitational force experienced by a test mass $m_0$ (in Newtons, N).
  • $m_0$ is the magnitude of the test mass (in kilograms, kg).

Crucially, the magnitude of the gravitational field strength is numerically equal to the acceleration due to gravity at that point, also denoted by $g$. So, $1 \text{ N/kg} = 1 \text{ m/s}^2$. The direction of the gravitational field at any point is the direction a free-falling object would accelerate.

3.2. Gravitational Field of a Point Mass or Spherical Body

For a single point mass $M$ (or a spherically symmetric body like a planet or star), the magnitude of the gravitational field strength at a distance $r$ from its center is:

$$g = G \frac{M}{r^2}$$

This formula explains why the acceleration due to gravity near the Earth's surface ($g \approx 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$) is roughly constant. It is the gravitational field strength at the Earth's surface ($r = \text{Earth's Radius}$). As you move farther from Earth, $r$ increases, and $g$ decreases according to the inverse square law.

Example: Gravitational Field on the Moon

The Moon has a mass of $7.34 \times 10^{22} \text{ kg}$ and a radius of $1.74 \times 10^6 \text{ m}$. Calculate the gravitational field strength on its surface.

Given: $M_{Moon} = 7.34 \times 10^{22} \text{ kg}$, $R_{Moon} = 1.74 \times 10^6 \text{ m}$, $G = 6.674 \times 10^{-11} \text{ NÂ·m}^2/\text{kg}^2$.

Using $g = G \frac{M}{r^2}$: $$g_{Moon} = (6.674 \times 10^{-11}) \frac{7.34 \times 10^{22}}{(1.74 \times 10^6)^2}$$ $$g_{Moon} = (6.674 \times 10^{-11}) \frac{7.34 \times 10^{22}}{3.0276 \times 10^{12}}$$ $$g_{Moon} \approx 1.62 \text{ N/kg} \text{ or } 1.62 \text{ m/s}^2$$ The gravitational field strength on the Moon's surface is approximately $1.62 \text{ m/s}^2$, which is about one-sixth of Earth's gravity. This explains why astronauts on the Moon could jump much higher.

4. Mass and Weight: A Crucial Distinction

In everyday language, "mass" and "weight" are often used interchangeably, but in physics, they are distinct concepts with different meanings and units.

4.1. Mass ($m$)

Mass ($m$) is a fundamental property of matter that quantifies the amount of "stuff" in an object and its inertia (resistance to changes in motion).

  • Mass is a scalar quantity.
  • The SI unit of mass is the kilogram (kg).
  • Mass is an intrinsic property of an object and does not change with location (e.g., your mass is the same on Earth, the Moon, or in deep space).

4.2. Weight ($W$)

Weight ($W$) is the force of gravity acting on an object. It is a vector quantity, always directed towards the center of the gravitational source.

$$W = mg$$

Where:

  • $W$ is the weight (in Newtons, N).
  • $m$ is the mass of the object (in kilograms, kg).
  • $g$ is the gravitational field strength (or acceleration due to gravity) at the object's location (in N/kg or m/s$^2$).

The SI unit of weight is the Newton (N), as it is a force.

  • Weight changes with location because $g$ changes with location. For example, your weight on the Moon is about one-sixth of your weight on Earth, even though your mass remains the same.
  • In deep space, far from any significant gravitational sources, your weight would be practically zero, but your mass would still be the same.

Example: Mass vs. Weight

A person has a mass of $80 \text{ kg}$.

  • Weight on Earth: Assuming $g_{Earth} = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$. $$W_{Earth} = mg_{Earth} = (80 \text{ kg})(9.8 \text{ m/s}^2) = 784 \text{ N}$$
  • Weight on Moon: Assuming $g_{Moon} = 1.62 \text{ m/s}^2$ (from previous example). $$W_{Moon} = mg_{Moon} = (80 \text{ kg})(1.62 \text{ m/s}^2) = 129.6 \text{ N}$$

The person's mass is always $80 \text{ kg}$, but their weight changes depending on the gravitational field.

4.3. Apparent Weight and Weightlessness

Apparent weight is the force an object exerts on its support (or the force a scale reads). It can differ from actual weight ($mg$) when there are additional vertical forces or acceleration.

  • In an Elevator:
    • Accelerating upward: Apparent weight > actual weight.
    • Accelerating downward: Apparent weight < actual weight.
    • Constant velocity: Apparent weight = actual weight.
    • Free fall (cable breaks): Apparent weight = 0 (this is true weightlessness).

Weightlessness does not mean an absence of gravity. Astronauts in orbit are constantly falling around Earth; they are in a state of continuous free fall, which creates the sensation of weightlessness (apparent weight is zero), even though Earth's gravitational field is still significant at their orbital altitude.

5. Gravitational Potential Energy

We previously defined gravitational potential energy ($PE_g = mgh$) for situations near the Earth's surface where $g$ is constant. However, for objects far from Earth, or for interactions between celestial bodies, $g$ is not constant, and we need a more general definition.

5.1. General Definition of Gravitational Potential Energy ($U_g$)

The gravitational potential energy ($U_g$) of a system of two masses $m_1$ and $m_2$ separated by a distance $r$ is defined as the work done by an external agent to bring the masses from infinite separation to their current separation, in the absence of other forces. Due to the nature of gravity, it's more convenient to define the zero potential energy point at infinite separation. This leads to a negative value for potential energy when masses are attracted.

$$U_g = -G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r}$$

Where:

  • $U_g$ is the gravitational potential energy (in Joules, J).
  • The negative sign indicates that the gravitational force is attractive. As $r$ decreases (masses get closer), $U_g$ becomes more negative, meaning the system is more stable or tightly bound.

This general formula is essential for understanding energy conservation in celestial mechanics, satellite orbits, and escape velocity.

5.2. Escape Velocity

Escape velocity ($v_e$) is the minimum speed an object must have to break free from the gravitational attraction of a massive body (like a planet or star) and never fall back. It is derived using the principle of conservation of energy. To escape, an object's total mechanical energy (kinetic + potential) must be zero or positive.

$$\frac{1}{2}mv_e^2 + U_g = 0$$ $$\frac{1}{2}mv_e^2 - G \frac{Mm}{R} = 0$$ $$v_e^2 = \frac{2GM}{R}$$ $$v_e = \sqrt{\frac{2GM}{R}}$$

Where:

  • $v_e$ is the escape velocity.
  • $G$ is the gravitational constant.
  • $M$ is the mass of the celestial body (e.g., Earth, Moon).
  • $R$ is the radius from the center of the celestial body from which the object is escaping.

For Earth, the escape velocity from the surface is approximately $11.2 \text{ km/s}$ ($25,000 \text{ mph}$). This is the speed required for rockets to leave Earth's gravity.

Example: Escape Velocity from Earth

Given Earth's mass $M_E = 5.97 \times 10^{24} \text{ kg}$ and radius $R_E = 6.37 \times 10^6 \text{ m}$, calculate the escape velocity from its surface.

Given: $M_E = 5.97 \times 10^{24} \text{ kg}$, $R_E = 6.37 \times 10^6 \text{ m}$, $G = 6.674 \times 10^{-11} \text{ NÂ·m}^2/\text{kg}^2$.

$$v_e = \sqrt{\frac{2GM_E}{R_E}}$$ $$v_e = \sqrt{\frac{2(6.674 \times 10^{-11})(5.97 \times 10^{24})}{6.37 \times 10^6}}$$ $$v_e = \sqrt{\frac{7.96 \times 10^{14}}{6.37 \times 10^6}} = \sqrt{1.249 \times 10^8}$$ $$v_e \approx 11175 \text{ m/s} \approx 11.2 \text{ km/s}$$

6. Basics of Planetary Motion: Kepler's Laws

Before Newton formulated his law of universal gravitation, Johannes Kepler, an astronomer, empirically derived three laws describing the motion of planets around the Sun based on Tycho Brahe's meticulous astronomical observations. Newton later showed that these empirical laws were a direct consequence of his gravitational law and the principles of classical mechanics.

6.1. Kepler's First Law: The Law of Orbits

"All planets move in elliptical orbits with the Sun at one focus."

This law states that orbits are not perfect circles but are ellipses. The Sun is not at the center of the ellipse, but at one of its two focal points.

6.2. Kepler's Second Law: The Law of Areas

"A line segment joining a planet and the Sun sweeps out equal areas during equal intervals of time."

This law implies that a planet moves faster when it is closer to the Sun (at perihelion) and slower when it is farther away (at aphelion). This is a consequence of the conservation of angular momentum.

6.3. Kepler's Third Law: The Law of Periods

"The square of the orbital period ($T$) of a planet is directly proportional to the cube of the semi-major axis ($a$) of its orbit."
$$T^2 \propto a^3$$

or, more precisely for orbits around a central mass $M$:

$$T^2 = \left(\frac{4\pi^2}{GM}\right) a^3$$

This law allows us to calculate the orbital period of a planet (or satellite) if we know the size of its orbit and the mass of the central body, or vice versa. It highlights a fundamental relationship between the orbital characteristics and the gravitational force.

Example: Kepler's Third Law

Mars's average distance from the Sun (semi-major axis) is approximately $1.52$ times Earth's average distance. Use Kepler's Third Law to estimate Mars's orbital period in Earth years.

Given: For Earth, $a_{Earth} = 1 \text{ AU}$ (Astronomical Unit), $T_{Earth} = 1 \text{ year}$. For Mars, $a_{Mars} = 1.52 \text{ AU}$.

From Kepler's Third Law: $T^2 \propto a^3$. So, $\frac{T_{Mars}^2}{T_{Earth}^2} = \frac{a_{Mars}^3}{a_{Earth}^3}$ $$T_{Mars}^2 = T_{Earth}^2 \left(\frac{a_{Mars}}{a_{Earth}}\right)^3$$ $$T_{Mars}^2 = (1 \text{ year})^2 \left(\frac{1.52 \text{ AU}}{1 \text{ AU}}\right)^3$$ $$T_{Mars}^2 = 1.52^3 \text{ year}^2 = 3.5118 \text{ year}^2$$ $$T_{Mars} = \sqrt{3.5118} \text{ years} \approx 1.87 \text{ years}$$ Mars's orbital period is approximately $1.87$ Earth years. This matches observed values remarkably well.

These laws are fundamental to understanding the stability and predictability of our solar system and the dynamics of exoplanetary systems.

8. Glossary of Key Terms

Acceleration due to Gravity ($g$): The acceleration experienced by an object due to a gravitational field. Numerically equal to gravitational field strength.
Apparent Weight: The force an object exerts on its support; can differ from actual weight due to acceleration.
Escape Velocity ($v_e$): The minimum speed an object needs to break free from a gravitational field and never fall back.
Gravitation: The universal attractive force between any two objects with mass.
Gravitational Field Strength ($\vec{g}$): The gravitational force per unit mass at a point in space. Measured in N/kg or m/s$^2$.
Gravitational Force ($\vec{F}_g$): The attractive force between two masses, described by Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation.
Gravitational Potential Energy ($U_g$): Energy stored in a system of masses due to their relative positions in a gravitational field ($U_g = -G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r}$). Defined as zero at infinite separation.
Inverse Square Law: A physical law stating that an intensity or force is inversely proportional to the square of the distance from the source.
Kepler's First Law (Law of Orbits): All planets move in elliptical orbits with the Sun at one focus.
Kepler's Second Law (Law of Areas): A line segment joining a planet and the Sun sweeps out equal areas during equal intervals of time.
Kepler's Third Law (Law of Periods): The square of the orbital period of a planet is proportional to the cube of the semi-major axis of its orbit ($T^2 \propto a^3$).
Mass ($m$): A fundamental measure of the amount of matter in an object and its inertia. Measured in kilograms (kg).
Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation: Every particle attracts every other particle with a force proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them ($F = G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2}$).
Planetary Motion: The movement of planets (or satellites) around a central massive body, governed by gravitation.
Universal Gravitational Constant ($G$): A fundamental constant in Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation, $6.674 \times 10^{-11} \text{ NÂ·m}^2/\text{kg}^2$.
Weight ($W$): The force of gravity acting on an object ($W = mg$). Measured in Newtons (N).
Weightlessness: A state where an object has no apparent weight, typically due to being in continuous free fall (e.g., in orbit).
