Momentum: Collisions and Conservation

Explore the fundamental concept of momentum and its conservation in various types of collisions. Master complex problems in one and two dimensions, including the coefficient of restitution.

1. Introduction to Momentum and Impulse

In physics, momentum is a crucial concept that quantifies the "quantity of motion" an object possesses. Unlike kinetic energy, which is a scalar quantity, momentum is a vector quantity, meaning it has both magnitude and direction. It plays a central role in analyzing interactions between objects, especially in collisions and explosions.

This lesson will begin by defining linear momentum and its close relative, impulse. We will then delve into one of the most powerful conservation laws in physics: the conservation of momentum. Finally, we'll apply these principles to various types of collisions, from simple one-dimensional impacts to complex two-dimensional interactions, and introduce a key parameter for collision analysis: the coefficient of restitution.

1.1 Linear Momentum ($\vec{p}$)

The linear momentum ($\vec{p}$) of an object is defined as the product of its mass ($m$) and its velocity ($\vec{v}$).

$$\vec{p} = m\vec{v}$$
  • $m$: mass of the object (in kilograms, kg).
  • $\vec{v}$: velocity of the object (in meters per second, m/s).
  • $\vec{p}$: linear momentum (in kilogram-meters per second, kgÂ·m/s).

Since velocity is a vector, momentum is also a vector, pointing in the same direction as the velocity. A heavier object moving at the same speed has more momentum than a lighter object. Similarly, an object with more speed has more momentum than the same object moving slower.

1.2 Relationship to Newton's Second Law

Newton originally formulated his Second Law in terms of momentum. He stated that the net force acting on an object is equal to the rate of change of its momentum:

$$\sum \vec{F} = \frac{d\vec{p}}{dt}$$

If mass is constant, this reduces to the more familiar $\sum \vec{F} = m\vec{a}$ (since $\vec{a} = \frac{d\vec{v}}{dt}$). However, the momentum form is more general, applicable even when mass changes (e.g., rocket propulsion).

1.3 Impulse ($\vec{J}$) and the Impulse-Momentum Theorem

When a force acts on an object for a period of time, it causes a change in the object's momentum. This change is quantified by impulse ($\vec{J}$). Impulse is defined as the integral of the net force over the time interval during which it acts:

$$\vec{J} = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \sum \vec{F} \, dt$$

If the net force is constant, this simplifies to:

$$\vec{J} = \sum \vec{F}_{avg} \Delta t$$

The unit of impulse is Newton-seconds (NÂ·s), which is equivalent to kgÂ·m/s.

The Impulse-Momentum Theorem states that the impulse imparted to an object is equal to the change in its linear momentum:

$$\vec{J} = \Delta \vec{p} = \vec{p}_{final} - \vec{p}_{initial} = m\vec{v}_{final} - m\vec{v}_{initial}$$

This theorem is incredibly useful for analyzing situations where forces act over short durations, such as collisions, impacts, and explosions, where the exact force profile might be complex but the change in momentum is readily measurable.

Example 1: Golf Ball Impact

A $0.045\, \text{kg}$ golf ball initially at rest is hit by a club. The club is in contact with the ball for $0.0005\, \text{s}$, and the ball leaves the club with a speed of $60\, \text{m/s}$.

a) Calculate the impulse imparted to the ball:

$\Delta \vec{p} = m\vec{v}_{final} - m\vec{v}_{initial} = (0.045\, \text{kg})(60\, \text{m/s}) - (0.045\, \text{kg})(0\, \text{m/s})$

$\Delta \vec{p} = 2.7\, \text{kg} \cdot \text{m/s}$

So, $\vec{J} = 2.7\, \text{N} \cdot \text{s}$ in the direction of the ball's final velocity.

b) Calculate the average force exerted by the club on the ball:

$\vec{J} = \vec{F}_{avg} \Delta t \Rightarrow \vec{F}_{avg} = \frac{\vec{J}}{\Delta t}$

$\vec{F}_{avg} = \frac{2.7\, \text{N} \cdot \text{s}}{0.0005\, \text{s}} = 5400\, \text{N}$

This large force over a short time demonstrates the power of impulse in impact events.

2. Conservation of Linear Momentum

The Principle of Conservation of Linear Momentum is one of the most fundamental and powerful laws in physics. It states that:

"If the net external force acting on a system of objects is zero, then the total linear momentum of the system remains constant."

Mathematically, for an isolated system:

$$\sum \vec{F}_{ext} = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \Delta \vec{P}_{total} = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \vec{P}_{initial} = \vec{P}_{final}$$

Where $\vec{P}_{total}$ is the vector sum of the momenta of all objects in the system. This means that the total momentum before an interaction (like a collision or an explosion) is equal to the total momentum after the interaction.

2.1 What is an "Isolated System"?

An isolated system is a system of objects where the net external force acting on the system is zero. It's crucial to distinguish between internal and external forces:

  • Internal Forces: Forces between objects within the system itself (e.g., collision forces between two cars in a system of two cars). These forces always form action-reaction pairs and cancel each other out within the system, so they do not change the total momentum of the system.
  • External Forces: Forces exerted on objects within the system by agents outside the system (e.g., friction from the road on the cars, gravity, air resistance). If the sum of these forces is zero (or negligible during a very short interaction like a collision), then the system is considered isolated with respect to momentum.

The conservation of momentum is a vector law, meaning it must hold true for each component (x, y, and z) independently.

Example 2: Recoiling Cannon

A $1000\, \text{kg}$ cannon fires a $5\, \text{kg}$ cannonball horizontally at $200\, \text{m/s}$. Assuming the cannon is initially at rest on a frictionless surface, what is the recoil velocity of the cannon?

System: Cannon + Cannonball. External forces (like friction) are negligible. Initial state: Both at rest. Total initial momentum $P_{initial} = 0$.

Final state: Cannonball moves forward, cannon recoils backward.

$m_{cannon} = 1000\, \text{kg}$, $v_{cannon, initial} = 0\, \text{m/s}$

$m_{ball} = 5\, \text{kg}$, $v_{ball, initial} = 0\, \text{m/s}$

$v_{ball, final} = +200\, \text{m/s}$ (let's define forward as positive)

$v_{cannon, final} = ?$

Conservation of Momentum: $P_{initial} = P_{final}$

$m_{cannon}v_{cannon, initial} + m_{ball}v_{ball, initial} = m_{cannon}v_{cannon, final} + m_{ball}v_{ball, final}$

$(1000)(0) + (5)(0) = (1000)v_{cannon, final} + (5)(200)$

$0 = 1000v_{cannon, final} + 1000$

$1000v_{cannon, final} = -1000$

$v_{cannon, final} = -1\, \text{m/s}$

The cannon recoils backward at $1\, \text{m/s}$.

3. Collisions in One Dimension

A collision is an event in which two or more objects exert forces on each other for a relatively short time, leading to a change in their momenta. During a collision, the forces involved are typically large, and any external forces (like gravity or friction) are often negligible over the short duration of the interaction, making momentum conservation a powerful tool.

Collisions are categorized based on whether kinetic energy is conserved:

3.1 Elastic Collisions

An elastic collision is a collision in which both linear momentum and kinetic energy are conserved. This means that the total kinetic energy of the system *before* the collision is equal to the total kinetic energy *after* the collision. In reality, perfectly elastic collisions are rare (e.g., collisions between atoms or perfectly hard billiard balls can approximate this).

For two objects $m_1$ and $m_2$ colliding elastically in one dimension:

Conservation of Momentum:

$$m_1 v_{1i} + m_2 v_{2i} = m_1 v_{1f} + m_2 v_{2f}$$

Conservation of Kinetic Energy:

$$\frac{1}{2} m_1 v_{1i}^2 + \frac{1}{2} m_2 v_{2i}^2 = \frac{1}{2} m_1 v_{1f}^2 + \frac{1}{2} m_2 v_{2f}^2$$

For 1D elastic collisions, a useful derived relationship for the relative velocities is:

$$v_{1i} - v_{2i} = -(v_{1f} - v_{2f}) \quad \Rightarrow \quad v_{1i} - v_{2i} = v_{2f} - v_{1f}$$

This means the relative speed of approach equals the relative speed of separation. This simplifies solving problems where two unknowns (final velocities) exist.

3.2 Inelastic Collisions

An inelastic collision is a collision in which linear momentum is conserved, but kinetic energy is *not* conserved. Some kinetic energy is transformed into other forms of energy, such as heat, sound, or energy of deformation. Most real-world collisions are inelastic to some degree.

Conservation of Momentum:

$$m_1 v_{1i} + m_2 v_{2i} = m_1 v_{1f} + m_2 v_{2f}$$

For inelastic collisions, you cannot use the conservation of kinetic energy equation. You must rely solely on momentum conservation (and sometimes other energy considerations if the energy loss is quantifiable).

3.3 Perfectly Inelastic Collisions

A perfectly inelastic collision is an extreme type of inelastic collision where the colliding objects stick together after the collision and move as a single combined mass. This type of collision results in the maximum possible loss of kinetic energy while still conserving momentum.

Conservation of Momentum:

$$m_1 v_{1i} + m_2 v_{2i} = (m_1 + m_2) v_f$$

Where $v_f$ is the common final velocity of the combined mass.

Example 3: Perfectly Inelastic Collision (1D)

A $5\, \text{kg}$ block moving at $10\, \text{m/s}$ collides with and sticks to a $3\, \text{kg}$ block initially at rest.

Given: $m_1 = 5\, \text{kg}$, $v_{1i} = 10\, \text{m/s}$, $m_2 = 3\, \text{kg}$, $v_{2i} = 0\, \text{m/s}$.

Conservation of Momentum:

$m_1 v_{1i} + m_2 v_{2i} = (m_1 + m_2) v_f$

$(5)(10) + (3)(0) = (5+3) v_f$

$50 = 8 v_f$

$v_f = \frac{50}{8} = 6.25\, \text{m/s}$

The combined mass moves at $6.25\, \text{m/s}$ after the collision.

Let's check kinetic energy:

$KE_{initial} = \frac{1}{2} m_1 v_{1i}^2 + \frac{1}{2} m_2 v_{2i}^2 = \frac{1}{2}(5)(10^2) + \frac{1}{2}(3)(0^2) = \frac{1}{2}(5)(100) = 250\, \text{J}$

$KE_{final} = \frac{1}{2} (m_1 + m_2) v_f^2 = \frac{1}{2}(8)(6.25^2) = 4(39.0625) = 156.25\, \text{J}$

Kinetic energy is lost ($250\, \text{J} - 156.25\, \text{J} = 93.75\, \text{J}$ lost), confirming it's an inelastic collision.

4. The Coefficient of Restitution ($\epsilon$)

The coefficient of restitution ($\epsilon$) is a dimensionless parameter that quantifies the elasticity of a collision. It provides a measure of how much kinetic energy is conserved (or lost) during an impact. It is defined as the ratio of the relative speed of separation after the collision to the relative speed of approach before the collision.

For a one-dimensional collision between two objects:

$$\epsilon = -\frac{(v_{1f} - v_{2f})}{(v_{1i} - v_{2i})} = \frac{\text{relative speed of separation}}{\text{relative speed of approach}}$$

The negative sign is included in the definition to ensure that $\epsilon$ is always positive, as $(v_{1f} - v_{2f})$ and $(v_{1i} - v_{2i})$ usually have opposite signs.

4.1 Values of $\epsilon$ and Collision Types

  • Perfectly Elastic Collision: $\epsilon = 1$
    The relative speed of separation equals the relative speed of approach. Kinetic energy is conserved. This is the maximum possible value for $\epsilon$.
  • Perfectly Inelastic Collision: $\epsilon = 0$
    The objects stick together after the collision, meaning their relative speed of separation is zero ($v_{1f} - v_{2f} = 0$). Kinetic energy loss is maximal.
  • Inelastic Collision: $0 < \epsilon < 1$
    Most real-world collisions fall into this category. Some kinetic energy is lost, but the objects do not stick together. The closer $\epsilon$ is to 1, the more elastic the collision.

The coefficient of restitution can be used in conjunction with the conservation of momentum equation to solve for two unknown final velocities in a 1D collision, providing an alternative to using the kinetic energy conservation equation for elastic collisions.

Example 4: Ball Bouncing Off a Wall

A ball approaches a wall with a speed of $5\, \text{m/s}$ and rebounds with a speed of $4\, \text{m/s}$. What is the coefficient of restitution between the ball and the wall?

Let the wall be object 2, and its velocity is $v_{2i} = v_{2f} = 0$. Let the initial direction of the ball be positive.

$v_{1i} = +5\, \text{m/s}$, $v_{1f} = -4\, \text{m/s}$ (opposite direction)

$\epsilon = -\frac{(v_{1f} - v_{2f})}{(v_{1i} - v_{2i})} = -\frac{(-4 - 0)}{(5 - 0)} = -\frac{-4}{5} = \frac{4}{5} = 0.8$

The coefficient of restitution is $0.8$, indicating an inelastic collision.

5. Collisions in Two Dimensions

When objects collide and their motion is not confined to a single line, we must analyze the collision in two dimensions (or three, for more complex cases). The principles of conservation of momentum and energy still apply, but their vector nature becomes critical.

5.1 Conservation of Momentum in 2D

Since momentum is a vector quantity, its conservation must hold true for each orthogonal component independently (x-axis and y-axis).

X-component of momentum conservation:

$$m_1 v_{1ix} + m_2 v_{2ix} = m_1 v_{1fx} + m_2 v_{2fx}$$

Y-component of momentum conservation:

$$m_1 v_{1iy} + m_2 v_{2iy} = m_1 v_{1fy} + m_2 v_{2fy}$$

Before applying these equations, it is essential to:

  • Choose a convenient coordinate system (e.g., x-axis horizontal, y-axis vertical).
  • Resolve all initial and final velocity vectors into their x and y components.

5.2 Types of 2D Collisions

5.2.1 Elastic Collisions in 2D

Similar to 1D, in a perfectly elastic 2D collision, both momentum (in x and y components) and kinetic energy are conserved.

Conservation of Kinetic Energy in 2D:

$$\frac{1}{2} m_1 v_{1i}^2 + \frac{1}{2} m_2 v_{2i}^2 = \frac{1}{2} m_1 v_{1f}^2 + \frac{1}{2} m_2 v_{2f}^2$$

Note that $v^2 = v_x^2 + v_y^2$. Solving 2D elastic collision problems can be complex, often requiring solving a system of three equations (one for KE, two for momentum components) with multiple unknowns.

5.2.2 Inelastic Collisions in 2D

For inelastic collisions in 2D, only momentum is conserved (in both components). Kinetic energy is not conserved.

5.2.3 Perfectly Inelastic Collisions in 2D

The objects stick together and move as a single combined mass after the collision.

Conservation of Momentum:

$$m_1 \vec{v}_{1i} + m_2 \vec{v}_{2i} = (m_1 + m_2) \vec{v}_f$$

Where $\vec{v}_f$ is the common final velocity vector of the combined mass. This equation can be split into x and y components.

Example 5: Two-Dimensional Perfectly Inelastic Collision

A $2.0\, \text{kg}$ ball ($m_1$) moving east at $4.0\, \text{m/s}$ collides with a $3.0\, \text{kg}$ ball ($m_2$) moving north at $2.0\, \text{m/s}$. They stick together after the collision. Find their final velocity.

Initial Velocities:

$\vec{v}_{1i} = (4.0\, \text{m/s})\hat{i}$

$\vec{v}_{2i} = (2.0\, \text{m/s})\hat{j}$

Conservation of Momentum (x-component):

$m_1 v_{1ix} + m_2 v_{2ix} = (m_1 + m_2) v_{fx}$

$(2.0\, \text{kg})(4.0\, \text{m/s}) + (3.0\, \text{kg})(0\, \text{m/s}) = (2.0\, \text{kg} + 3.0\, \text{kg}) v_{fx}$

$8.0 = 5.0 v_{fx} \Rightarrow v_{fx} = 1.6\, \text{m/s}$

Conservation of Momentum (y-component):

$m_1 v_{1iy} + m_2 v_{2iy} = (m_1 + m_2) v_{fy}$

$(2.0\, \text{kg})(0\, \text{m/s}) + (3.0\, \text{kg})(2.0\, \text{m/s}) = (2.0\, \text{kg} + 3.0\, \text{kg}) v_{fy}$

$6.0 = 5.0 v_{fy} \Rightarrow v_{fy} = 1.2\, \text{m/s}$

Final Velocity Vector: $\vec{v}_f = (1.6\, \text{m/s})\hat{i} + (1.2\, \text{m/s})\hat{j}$

Magnitude: $|\vec{v}_f| = \sqrt{1.6^2 + 1.2^2} = \sqrt{2.56 + 1.44} = \sqrt{4.00} = 2.0\, \text{m/s}$

Direction: $\phi = \arctan\left(\frac{1.2}{1.6}\right) = \arctan(0.75) \approx 36.87^\circ$ North of East.

6. Complex Collision Problems and Systems

Real-world scenarios often involve more than two objects or a sequence of collisions. Analyzing these situations requires a systematic application of the conservation laws, often by defining appropriate systems and analyzing interactions step-by-step.

6.1 Multi-Object Collisions

The conservation of momentum applies to any system of particles, regardless of the number of objects involved. For a system of $N$ objects, the total initial momentum is equal to the total final momentum:

$$\sum_{i=1}^{N} m_i \vec{v}_{ii} = \sum_{i=1}^{N} m_i \vec{v}_{if}$$

This is particularly useful for explosions (where a single object breaks into multiple pieces) or chain reactions of collisions.

6.2 Collisions with External Forces

While the principle of conservation of momentum strictly applies only to isolated systems, it can still be used effectively for systems where external forces are present, provided the collision duration is very short. In such cases, the external forces are often negligible compared to the large internal collision forces (impulsive forces).

If external forces cannot be neglected (e.g., a collision over a long duration where friction plays a significant role), then the Impulse-Momentum Theorem ($\vec{J}_{ext} = \Delta \vec{P}_{total}$) should be used, where $\vec{J}_{ext}$ is the impulse due to external forces.

6.3 Center of Mass and Collisions

The center of mass (CM) of a system of objects is a unique point that represents the average position of all the mass in the system. An important property is that the total momentum of a system is equal to the total mass of the system multiplied by the velocity of its center of mass ($\vec{P}_{total} = M_{total} \vec{v}_{CM}$).

During a collision (whether elastic or inelastic), if there are no net external forces, the velocity of the center of mass remains constant. The individual objects within the system may change their velocities, but the "average" motion of the system as a whole (represented by its CM) continues uninterrupted. This provides a powerful way to check calculations and understand collision dynamics.

7. Applications and Advanced Topics in Momentum

The principles of momentum and collisions are not just theoretical constructs; they are vital for understanding and engineering a vast array of real-world phenomena and technologies.

7.1 Safety and Design

Understanding impulse is crucial in designing safety features:

  • Airbags: Increase the time of impact during a collision, thereby reducing the average force exerted on an occupant (since $\vec{F}_{avg} = \frac{\Delta \vec{p}}{\Delta t}$).
  • Crumple Zones: In cars, designed to deform during a crash, extending the collision time and reducing forces on occupants.
  • Cushioning Materials: Used in packaging, sports equipment (helmets, pads), and playgrounds to absorb impact energy and reduce forces.

7.2 Sports and Recreation

Momentum is fundamental to many sports:

  • Billiards/Pool: Demonstrates elastic collisions and momentum transfer.
  • Baseball/Tennis/Golf: The impulse imparted by a bat/racket/club to a ball.
  • Football/Rugby: Tackles and impacts involve momentum exchange.

7.3 Rocket Propulsion

The principle of rocket propulsion is a direct application of the conservation of momentum. A rocket expels high-velocity exhaust gases backward (mass expelled, momentum in one direction), and in response, the rocket gains momentum forward (recoil). This occurs even in the vacuum of space, as no external medium is needed for the exhaust to push against.

7.4 Particle Physics

In particle physics, collisions between subatomic particles are central to research. Analyzing these collisions, often at extremely high energies, relies entirely on the conservation of momentum and energy (including mass-energy equivalence from relativity). Experiments in particle accelerators (like the LHC) are essentially highly controlled collision events.

7.5 Ballistic Pendulum

The ballistic pendulum is a classic physics experiment used to determine the speed of a projectile (like a bullet). It involves a perfectly inelastic collision (bullet embedding in a block) followed by the block-bullet system swinging upwards, allowing the initial speed to be calculated using a combination of momentum conservation (during the collision) and energy conservation (during the swing).
