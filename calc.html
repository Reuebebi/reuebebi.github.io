Calculus: The Mathematics of Change

Calculus helps us understand how things change and move. Whether it's the speed of a car, the growth of a population, or the slope of a mountain, calculus provides tools to measure and analyze these changes.

1. Introduction to Limits

A limit shows how a function behaves as it gets close to a certain point. For example, if we look at a graph and see where the line heads as x gets close to 2, we're looking at a limit.

Example: What happens to (x² - 1)/(x - 1) as x approaches 1?

It looks undefined at x = 1, but we can simplify it to x + 1. So the limit is 2.

2. Continuity

A function is continuous if we can draw it without lifting our pencil. That means no holes, jumps, or gaps in the graph.

To be continuous at a point, the function must be defined there, the limit must exist, and both must be equal.

3. Derivatives: Measuring Instant Change

Derivatives help us measure how fast something is changing at a specific point. It's like asking, "How fast is my speed changing at this moment?"

The derivative is the slope of the function at a point. You can think of it as the speed on a car’s speedometer.

Formula: f'(x) = limₕ→0 [f(x + h) - f(x)] / h

Basic Rules:

  • Power Rule: d/dx[xⁿ] = n*xⁿ⁻¹
  • Product Rule: d/dx[uv] = u'v + uv'
  • Quotient Rule: d/dx[u/v] = (u'v - uv') / v²
  • Chain Rule: d/dx[f(g(x))] = f'(g(x)) * g'(x)

4. Applications of Derivatives

We can use derivatives to:

  • Find where functions increase or decrease
  • Determine maximum or minimum values
  • Analyze motion: speed, velocity, and acceleration
  • Sketch graphs accurately

5. Integrals: Measuring Area and Accumulation

Integrals help us add up tiny pieces to find a whole. They're used to find areas, total distances, and even volume.

Symbol: ∫ f(x) dx

  • Indefinite Integrals: No limits, general formula + C
  • Definite Integrals: With limits, gives exact value

6. Integration Techniques

Just like there are rules for derivatives, there are strategies for solving integrals:

  • Substitution: Change variables to simplify
  • Integration by Parts: Based on product rule
  • Partial Fractions: Break into simpler fractions
  • Trigonometric Substitution: Use trig identities

7. Real-Life Applications of Integration

  • Finding the area under a curve (like distance from speed)
  • Calculating volumes of 3D shapes
  • Finding total accumulated values

8. The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

This connects derivatives and integrals. It says that integration and differentiation are opposites.

If F is the antiderivative of f, then:

∫ₐᵇ f(x) dx = F(b) - F(a)

9. Differential Equations

These are equations with derivatives. They help us model change over time, like population growth or cooling of an object.

dy/dx = ky → y = Ce^(kx)

10. Practical Uses of Calculus

Calculus isn't just for math class. It's used in:

  • Physics: motion, forces, electricity
  • Economics: cost and profit analysis
  • Biology: growth and decay models
  • Engineering: systems and optimization
