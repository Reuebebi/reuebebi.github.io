2. Fundamental Characteristics of Waves

A wave is a disturbance that propagates through space and time, usually by the transfer of energy without the net transfer of matter. Think of a ripple in a pond: the water oscillates up and down, but the disturbance (the wave) travels outwards.

2.1. Types of Waves

Waves can be broadly classified based on the direction of oscillation of the medium's particles relative to the direction of wave propagation:

  • Transverse Waves: The particles of the medium oscillate perpendicular to the direction of wave propagation. Examples include waves on a string, electromagnetic waves (like light), and ocean waves.
  • Longitudinal Waves: The particles of the medium oscillate parallel to the direction of wave propagation. These waves consist of compressions (regions of high density/pressure) and rarefactions (regions of low density/pressure). Sound waves are the most common example of longitudinal waves.

Another classification is based on whether a medium is required:

  • Mechanical Waves: Require a medium to travel through (e.g., sound waves, water waves, waves on a spring).
  • Electromagnetic Waves: Do not require a medium and can travel through a vacuum (e.g., light, radio waves, X-rays). These are self-propagating oscillations of electric and magnetic fields.

2.2. Key Wave Properties

To describe waves quantitatively, we use several key properties:

  • Amplitude ($A$): The maximum displacement or distance moved by a point on a vibrating body or wave measured from its equilibrium position. For a water wave, it's the height from the equilibrium level to a crest or trough. For a sound wave, it's related to the pressure variation. Amplitude is related to the energy carried by the wave.
  • Wavelength ($\lambda$): The spatial period of the wave, the distance over which the wave's shape repeats. It is the distance between two consecutive corresponding points on a wave, such as two adjacent crests or two adjacent troughs. Measured in meters (m).
  • Frequency ($f$): The number of complete oscillations or cycles that pass a given point per unit of time. Measured in Hertz (Hz), where $1 \text{ Hz} = 1 \text{ cycle/second}$. Frequency is determined by the source of the wave.
  • Period ($T$): The time it takes for one complete oscillation or cycle to pass a given point. It is the reciprocal of frequency. Measured in seconds (s).
    $$T = \frac{1}{f}$$
  • Wave Speed ($v$): The speed at which the wave propagates through the medium. It's the distance a wave travels per unit of time.

2.3. The Wave Speed Equation

The wave speed, wavelength, and frequency are intimately related by a fundamental equation:

$$v = f\lambda$$

Where:

  • $v$ is the wave speed (in m/s).
  • $f$ is the frequency (in Hz).
  • $\lambda$ is the wavelength (in m).

This equation tells us that if a wave has a high frequency, it must have a short wavelength to maintain a certain speed, and vice-versa. The speed of a wave is determined by the properties of the medium through which it travels, not by its source's frequency or amplitude.

Example: Wave Speed Calculation

A sound wave in air has a frequency of $440 \text{ Hz}$ (Concert A) and a wavelength of $0.78 \text{ m}$. What is the speed of sound in air?

Given: $f = 440 \text{ Hz}$, $\lambda = 0.78 \text{ m}$.

Using $v = f\lambda$: $$v = (440 \text{ Hz})(0.78 \text{ m})$$ $$v = 343.2 \text{ m/s}$$ The speed of sound in air is approximately $343 \text{ m/s}$.

3. Basic Properties of Light

Light is a specific type of electromagnetic wave, which is a transverse wave consisting of oscillating electric and magnetic fields that propagate perpendicular to each other and to the direction of energy transfer. Unlike mechanical waves, light does not require a medium and can travel through the vacuum of space.

3.1. The Electromagnetic Spectrum

Visible light is just a small portion of the vast electromagnetic spectrum, which includes (in order of increasing frequency and decreasing wavelength): radio waves, microwaves, infrared radiation, visible light, ultraviolet radiation, X-rays, and gamma rays. All electromagnetic waves travel at the same speed in a vacuum.

3.2. Speed of Light in Vacuum ($c$)

The speed of light in a vacuum is a fundamental physical constant, denoted by $c$. Its value is approximately:

$$c = 299,792,458 \text{ m/s} \approx 3.00 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}$$

This incredibly high speed is why light seems to travel instantaneously over short distances. In transparent media (like water or glass), light travels slower than $c$.

3.3. Wave-Particle Duality (Brief Mention)

One of the most intriguing aspects of light is its wave-particle duality. While light undeniably exhibits wave-like properties (like interference and diffraction, which we'll briefly mention), it also behaves like a particle, called a photon, in certain interactions (like the photoelectric effect). For most introductory optics, treating light as a wave is sufficient.

4. Reflection: Bouncing Back

Reflection is the phenomenon where a wave, such as light, bounces off a surface and changes direction. It's how we see objects (light reflects off them into our eyes) and how mirrors work.

4.1. Law of Reflection

The Law of Reflection is a simple yet powerful rule that governs how light reflects from a smooth surface. It states:

  • The incident ray (incoming light ray), the reflected ray (outgoing light ray), and the normal (a line perpendicular to the surface at the point of incidence) all lie in the same plane.
  • The angle of incidence ($\theta_i$) is equal to the angle of reflection ($\theta_r$).
    $$\theta_i = \theta_r$$
    These angles are always measured with respect to the normal.

4.2. Types of Reflection

The appearance of a reflected image depends on the nature of the reflecting surface:

  • Specular (Regular) Reflection: Occurs when light reflects off a smooth, polished surface (like a mirror or still water). Parallel incident rays reflect as parallel reflected rays, forming clear, distinct images.
  • Diffuse Reflection: Occurs when light reflects off a rough or uneven surface (like a wall or clothing). Parallel incident rays scatter in many different directions, which is why we can see objects from various angles and why rough surfaces don't produce clear reflections. Most objects we see are due to diffuse reflection.

4.3. Mirrors (Brief Mention)

Mirrors are surfaces that produce specular reflection.

  • Plane Mirrors: Flat mirrors that produce virtual (light rays do not actually converge at the image), upright, same-size images that are laterally inverted (left and right are swapped) and appear to be behind the mirror at the same distance as the object in front.
  • Spherical Mirrors: Mirrors that are segments of a sphere.
    • Concave Mirrors: Curved inward (like a spoon). They converge parallel light rays to a focal point. Can form both real and virtual images, depending on object position. Used in shaving mirrors, headlights, and telescopes.
    • Convex Mirrors: Curved outward. They diverge parallel light rays, making objects appear smaller and farther away. Always form virtual, upright, and diminished images. Used in side-view mirrors on cars and security mirrors in stores to provide a wider field of view.

5. Refraction: Bending Through Media

Refraction is the bending of a wave as it passes from one medium into another, due to a change in the wave's speed. When light passes from air into water, for example, its speed changes, causing it to bend. This phenomenon is responsible for why objects submerged in water appear distorted, and how lenses work.

5.1. Index of Refraction ($n$)

The index of refraction ($n$) of a medium is a dimensionless quantity that describes how much light slows down when it enters that medium. It is defined as the ratio of the speed of light in a vacuum ($c$) to the speed of light in the medium ($v$):

$$n = \frac{c}{v}$$

The index of refraction is always greater than or equal to 1. For a vacuum, $n=1$. For air, $n \approx 1.0003$. For water, $n \approx 1.33$. For glass, $n$ typically ranges from $1.5$ to $1.9$. A higher index of refraction means light travels slower in that medium and bends more when entering or leaving it.

5.2. Snell's Law

Snell's Law (also known as the Law of Refraction) quantitatively describes the relationship between the angles of incidence and refraction when light passes between two different media.

$$n_1 \sin(\theta_1) = n_2 \sin(\theta_2)$$

Where:

  • $n_1$ is the index of refraction of the first medium.
  • $\theta_1$ is the angle of incidence (angle between the incident ray and the normal to the surface).
  • $n_2$ is the index of refraction of the second medium.
  • $\theta_2$ is the angle of refraction (angle between the refracted ray and the normal in the second medium).

Key implications of Snell's Law:

  • If light passes from a less dense medium ($n_1$) to a more dense medium ($n_2$) (i.e., $n_1 < n_2$), then $\theta_2 < \theta_1$, and the light ray bends towards the normal.
  • If light passes from a more dense medium ($n_1$) to a less dense medium ($n_2$) (i.e., $n_1 > n_2$), then $\theta_2 > \theta_1$, and the light ray bends away from the normal.
  • If light hits the boundary perpendicularly ($\theta_1 = 0^\circ$), it does not bend, as $\sin(0^\circ) = 0$.

Example: Snell's Law Application

A light ray passes from air ($n_1 = 1.00$) into water ($n_2 = 1.33$) at an angle of incidence of $30^\circ$. What is the angle of refraction?

Given: $n_1 = 1.00$, $\theta_1 = 30^\circ$, $n_2 = 1.33$.

Using Snell's Law: $n_1 \sin(\theta_1) = n_2 \sin(\theta_2)$ $$1.00 \sin(30^\circ) = 1.33 \sin(\theta_2)$$ $$1.00 \times 0.5 = 1.33 \sin(\theta_2)$$ $$0.5 = 1.33 \sin(\theta_2)$$ $$\sin(\theta_2) = \frac{0.5}{1.33} \approx 0.376$$ $$\theta_2 = \arcsin(0.376) \approx 22.1^\circ$$ The angle of refraction is approximately $22.1^\circ$. The light bends towards the normal, as expected when entering a denser medium.

5.3. Total Internal Reflection (Brief Mention)

When light travels from a denser medium to a less dense medium (e.g., from water to air), there is a critical angle. If the angle of incidence exceeds this critical angle, the light ray will not refract into the second medium but will instead be entirely reflected back into the first medium. This phenomenon is called Total Internal Reflection (TIR) and is the principle behind fiber optics, diamonds' sparkle, and binoculars.

5.4. Lenses (Brief Mention)

Lenses are optical devices that use refraction to focus or disperse light, forming images. They are typically made of transparent materials like glass or plastic, with curved surfaces.

  • Converging Lenses (Convex): Thicker in the middle than at the edges. They converge parallel light rays to a real focal point. Used in magnifying glasses, cameras, and corrective lenses for farsightedness.
  • Diverging Lenses (Concave): Thinner in the middle than at the edges. They diverge parallel light rays, making them appear to originate from a virtual focal point. Used in peepholes and corrective lenses for nearsightedness.

6. Wave Phenomena: Interference and Diffraction

Beyond reflection and refraction, waves exhibit other fascinating behaviors that provide strong evidence for their wave nature.

6.1. Interference

Interference occurs when two or more waves overlap, resulting in a new wave pattern whose amplitude is the sum of the amplitudes of the individual waves.

  • Constructive Interference: Occurs when waves combine "in phase" (crest meets crest, trough meets trough), resulting in a larger amplitude.
  • Destructive Interference: Occurs when waves combine "out of phase" (crest meets trough), resulting in a smaller or even zero amplitude.

Examples include standing waves on a string, beats produced by two slightly different sound frequencies, and the vibrant colors seen in soap bubbles or oil slicks.

6.2. Diffraction

Diffraction is the bending of waves around obstacles or the spreading out of waves as they pass through narrow openings. This phenomenon is why you can hear sound around a corner even if you can't see the source, and why light passing through a tiny slit produces a spreading pattern instead of a sharp image.

The amount of diffraction is most significant when the wavelength of the wave is comparable to the size of the obstacle or opening. This is why sound waves (long wavelengths) diffract easily around everyday objects, while visible light waves (short wavelengths) do not, unless the opening is very small.

8. Glossary of Key Terms

Amplitude ($A$): The maximum displacement of a wave from its equilibrium position.
Angle of Incidence ($\theta_i$): The angle between an incident ray and the normal to the surface.
Angle of Reflection ($\theta_r$): The angle between a reflected ray and the normal to the surface. Equal to the angle of incidence.
Angle of Refraction ($\theta_2$): The angle between a refracted ray and the normal in the second medium.
Concave Mirror/Lens: Curved inward mirror (converges light) or lens (diverges light).
Converging Lens (Convex): A lens that focuses parallel light rays to a point.
Convex Mirror/Lens: Curved outward mirror (diverges light) or lens (converges light).
Critical Angle: The angle of incidence above which total internal reflection occurs.
Diffraction: The bending of waves around obstacles or through narrow openings.
Diverging Lens (Concave): A lens that spreads out parallel light rays.
Electromagnetic Spectrum: The full range of electromagnetic waves, from radio waves to gamma rays.
Electromagnetic Wave: A transverse wave consisting of oscillating electric and magnetic fields; does not require a medium (e.g., light).
Frequency ($f$): The number of wave cycles passing a point per unit time. Measured in Hertz (Hz).
Incident Ray: The incoming light ray.
Index of Refraction ($n$): A measure of how much light slows down in a medium ($n = c/v$).
Interference: The phenomenon where two or more waves overlap to form a new wave pattern.
Law of Reflection: States that the angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection ($\theta_i = \theta_r$).
Lenses: Optical devices that use refraction to focus or disperse light.
Light: The portion of the electromagnetic spectrum that is visible to the human eye.
Longitudinal Wave: A wave in which particles of the medium oscillate parallel to the wave propagation direction (e.g., sound).
Mechanical Wave: A wave that requires a medium to travel (e.g., sound, water waves).
Normal: A line perpendicular to a surface at the point where a ray strikes it.
Optics: The branch of physics that studies the behavior and properties of light.
Period ($T$): The time for one complete wave cycle ($T = 1/f$).
Photon: A quantum of light, behaving as a particle.
Plane Mirror: A flat mirror that produces virtual, upright, same-size, laterally inverted images.
Reflected Ray: The outgoing light ray after reflection.
Reflection: The bouncing back of a wave from a surface.
Refraction: The bending of a wave as it passes from one medium to another.
Snell's Law: Describes refraction: $n_1 \sin(\theta_1) = n_2 \sin(\theta_2)$.
Specular Reflection: Reflection from a smooth surface, producing clear images.
Speed of Light in Vacuum ($c$): Approximately $3.00 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}$.
Total Internal Reflection (TIR): Complete reflection of a light ray back into a denser medium when the angle of incidence exceeds the critical angle.
Transverse Wave: A wave in which particles of the medium oscillate perpendicular to the wave propagation direction (e.g., light, water waves).
Wave: A disturbance that propagates through space and time, transferring energy.
Wave Speed ($v$): The speed at which a wave travels through a medium ($v = f\lambda$).
Wavelength ($\lambda$): The spatial period of a wave, the distance between two consecutive corresponding points on a wave. Measured in meters (m).
