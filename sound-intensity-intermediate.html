Sound as a Wave and Its Power

Sound is a mechanical wave that travels through a medium by causing particles of the medium to oscillate. It is typically a longitudinal wave, meaning the oscillations of the medium's particles are parallel to the direction of wave propagation.

Sound and Energy Transfer

Like all waves, sound waves carry energy from a source to a receiver. As sound propagates, it causes vibrations in the medium, transferring kinetic and potential energy to the particles. The louder the sound, the more energy it carries.

Sound Power ($P$)

The sound power ($P$) of a source is the total amount of sound energy emitted by the source per unit time. It is a fundamental property of the source itself, independent of distance or environment.

  • Unit: Watt (W), where $1 \text{ W} = 1 \text{ Joule/second (J/s)}$.
  • A sound source might have a power output of a few microwatts for a quiet whisper to hundreds of watts for a loud speaker system.

Sound Intensity ($I$)

Sound intensity ($I$) is the average rate at which sound energy flows through a unit area perpendicular to the direction of wave propagation. It quantifies how much sound power is concentrated in a given area. It's what our ears primarily perceive as loudness. $$ I = \frac{P}{A} $$ Where:

  • $I$: Sound intensity.
  • $P$: Sound power from the source.
  • $A$: Area over which the power is distributed.

Unit: The SI unit of sound intensity is Watts per square meter ($\text{W/m}^2$).

  • The threshold of human hearing (the faintest sound a young, healthy ear can detect) is approximately $I_0 = 10^{-12} \text{ W/m}^2$.
  • The threshold of pain is around $1 \text{ W/m}^2$. This represents an enormous range of intensities.

Relationship with Amplitude

Sound intensity is proportional to the square of the sound wave's amplitude (specifically, the pressure amplitude or displacement amplitude). $$ I \propto (\text{pressure amplitude})^2 \quad \text{or} \quad I \propto (\text{displacement amplitude})^2 $$ This means that a small increase in amplitude leads to a much larger increase in intensity.

The Decibel Scale: Quantifying Loudness

Because the range of sound intensities that the human ear can perceive is so vast (from $10^{-12} \text{ W/m}^2$ to $1 \text{ W/m}^2$, a factor of $10^{12}$), a linear scale is impractical. Instead, a logarithmic scale called the decibel (dB) scale is used. This scale compresses the enormous range of intensities into a more manageable set of numbers, and it also aligns better with how humans perceive loudness, which is logarithmic.

Sound Intensity Level ($\beta$)

The sound intensity level ($\beta$) in decibels is defined as: $$ \beta = 10 \log_{10}\left(\frac{I}{I_0}\right) $$ Where:

  • $\beta$: Sound intensity level (in decibels, dB).
  • $I$: Measured sound intensity.
  • $I_0$: Reference intensity, which is typically the threshold of human hearing, $I_0 = 10^{-12} \text{ W/m}^2$.
  • $\log_{10}$: Logarithm to the base 10.

Properties of the Decibel Scale

  • Logarithmic: Every increase of 10 dB corresponds to a tenfold increase in sound intensity.
    • +10 dB means 10 times the intensity.
    • +20 dB means 100 times the intensity.
    • +30 dB means 1000 times the intensity.
  • Perception: A 10 dB increase is generally perceived by humans as roughly a doubling in loudness.
  • Thresholds:
    • Threshold of hearing ($I_0 = 10^{-12} \text{ W/m}^2$): $\beta = 10 \log_{10}(10^{-12}/10^{-12}) = 10 \log_{10}(1) = 0 \text{ dB}$.
    • Threshold of pain ($I = 1 \text{ W/m}^2$): $\beta = 10 \log_{10}(1/10^{-12}) = 10 \log_{10}(10^{12}) = 10 \times 12 = 120 \text{ dB}$.

Inverse Square Law for Sound Propagation

For a point source emitting sound uniformly in all directions (isotropically) in an open, unbounded medium without reflections, the sound energy spreads out spherically. As the wavefront expands, the same amount of power is distributed over an increasingly larger area. This leads to the inverse square law for sound intensity.

Derivation and Formula

Consider a point source emitting sound with power $P$. At a distance $r$ from the source, the sound energy passes through a spherical surface with area $A = 4\pi r^2$. The intensity $I$ at this distance is: $$ I = \frac{P}{A} = \frac{P}{4\pi r^2} $$

This equation shows that sound intensity is inversely proportional to the square of the distance from the source ($I \propto 1/r^2$).

If we have two distances, $r_1$ and $r_2$, from the same sound source, the intensities $I_1$ and $I_2$ at these distances are related by: $$ I_1 r_1^2 = I_2 r_2^2 \quad \text{ or } \quad \frac{I_1}{I_2} = \left(\frac{r_2}{r_1}\right)^2 $$

In terms of Decibels: Since the decibel scale is logarithmic, the relationship for sound intensity levels at different distances is: $$ \beta_2 - \beta_1 = 10 \log_{10}\left(\frac{I_2}{I_1}\right) $$ Using the inverse square law, $\frac{I_2}{I_1} = \left(\frac{r_1}{r_2}\right)^2$, we get: $$ \beta_2 - \beta_1 = 10 \log_{10}\left(\left(\frac{r_1}{r_2}\right)^2\right) $$ $$ \beta_2 - \beta_1 = 20 \log_{10}\left(\frac{r_1}{r_2}\right) $$ This means that for every doubling of distance, the sound intensity level drops by 6 dB ($20 \log_{10}(1/2) \approx -6 \text{ dB}$).

Limitations and Real-World Factors

The inverse square law is an idealization. In real-world environments, several factors can cause deviations:

  • Absorption: The medium itself absorbs some sound energy, especially at higher frequencies.
  • Reflections: Walls, ceilings, and other objects reflect sound, preventing it from spreading uniformly and often increasing intensity in certain areas (reverberation).
  • Diffraction: Sound waves bend around obstacles, distributing energy into shadowed regions.
  • Atmospheric Conditions: Wind, temperature gradients, and humidity can affect sound propagation.
  • Directional Sources: Many sound sources are not isotropic (e.g., a loudspeaker directs sound in a specific direction), which alters the energy distribution.

Despite these complexities, the inverse square law provides a fundamental understanding of how sound intensity generally behaves in free space and serves as a starting point for more complex acoustic modeling.

Resonance

Resonance is a phenomenon that occurs when an oscillating system (or an object with a natural frequency) is driven by an external force or wave at or near one of its natural frequencies. At resonance, the system absorbs energy most efficiently, leading to a dramatic increase in the amplitude of its oscillations.

Natural Frequency

Every object or system capable of oscillating has one or more natural frequencies (also called resonant frequencies or eigenfrequencies). These are the frequencies at which the object will oscillate freely if disturbed and then left alone. For example, a pendulum has a natural frequency determined by its length, and a guitar string has natural frequencies determined by its length, tension, and mass.

The Resonance Phenomenon

  • When an external periodic force (or wave) acts on a system, if the frequency of this driving force matches a natural frequency of the system, resonance occurs.
  • At resonance, the system effectively "stores" the energy from the driving force, and the amplitude of its oscillations grows significantly.
  • If damping (energy dissipation) is low, the amplitude can become very large.

Examples of Resonance

  • Swing Set: Pushing a child on a swing at just the right time (its natural frequency) causes the swing to go higher and higher with minimal effort. Pushing at other frequencies is inefficient.
  • Musical Instruments: Instruments are designed to resonate at specific frequencies to produce musical notes.
    • Blowing across a bottle causes the air column inside to resonate, producing a sound.
    • Striking a tuning fork causes its prongs to vibrate at its natural frequency. If you place its base on a surface, the surface can resonate, amplifying the sound.
    • The body of a guitar or violin acts as a resonator, amplifying the sound produced by the vibrating strings.
  • Tacoma Narrows Bridge (Galloping Gertie): A famous historical example of destructive resonance. Wind gusts caused the bridge to oscillate at its natural torsional (twisting) frequency, leading to its catastrophic collapse in 1940. This event dramatically improved understanding of structural resonance.
  • Microwave Ovens: Microwave ovens operate by generating electromagnetic waves at a frequency (around 2.45 GHz) that causes water molecules in food to resonate and vibrate rapidly, generating heat.
  • Radio and TV Tuning: When you tune a radio or TV, you are adjusting its internal electronic circuit to resonate at the frequency of the desired broadcasting station, allowing it to efficiently receive that signal.
  • MRI (Magnetic Resonance Imaging): Uses radio waves to excite protons in the body, which then resonate and emit signals that can be detected to create detailed images of internal tissues.

Resonance is a powerful phenomenon that is harnessed in countless beneficial technologies, but it must also be carefully considered in engineering design to prevent destructive oscillations.

Standing Waves in Pipes and Strings

As briefly discussed in the "Wave Phenomena" lesson, standing waves are stationary wave patterns formed when two waves of the same frequency and amplitude travel in opposite directions and interfere. They are crucial to understanding how musical instruments produce their characteristic notes.

Key Elements: Nodes and Antinodes

  • Nodes: Points of zero displacement (or pressure variation for sound waves in pipes). These are points of complete destructive interference.
  • Antinodes: Points of maximum displacement (or pressure variation). These are points of complete constructive interference.

The distance between two consecutive nodes (or antinodes) is always half a wavelength ($\lambda/2$). The distance between a node and an adjacent antinode is a quarter wavelength ($\lambda/4$).

Standing Waves on a String Fixed at Both Ends

This model applies to string instruments like guitars, violins, and pianos. Since the ends are fixed, they must be nodes (points of zero displacement).

  • The simplest standing wave (the fundamental frequency or first harmonic, $n=1$) has one antinode in the middle and nodes at both ends. Its wavelength is $2L$, where $L$ is the length of the string.
  • Higher harmonics ($n=2, 3, 4, \dots$) have more antinodes and nodes.
  • Possible Wavelengths ($\lambda_n$): $$ L = n \frac{\lambda_n}{2} \implies \lambda_n = \frac{2L}{n} \quad (n = 1, 2, 3, \dots) $$
  • Resonant Frequencies ($f_n$): Using $f = v/\lambda$, where $v$ is the wave speed on the string (determined by tension and mass per unit length): $$ f_n = \frac{v}{\lambda_n} = \frac{nv}{2L} \quad (n = 1, 2, 3, \dots) $$ The fundamental frequency is $f_1 = v/(2L)$.

Standing Waves in Open Pipes (Open at Both Ends)

This model applies to wind instruments like flutes and some organ pipes. At an open end of an air column, particles of air can move freely, so an antinode (maximum displacement/pressure variation minimum) must form.

  • The simplest standing wave ($n=1$) has an antinode at each end and one node in the middle. Its wavelength is $2L$.
  • Possible Wavelengths ($\lambda_n$): $$ L = n \frac{\lambda_n}{2} \implies \lambda_n = \frac{2L}{n} \quad (n = 1, 2, 3, \dots) $$
  • Resonant Frequencies ($f_n$): Using $f = v/\lambda$, where $v$ is the speed of sound in air: $$ f_n = \frac{v}{\lambda_n} = \frac{nv}{2L} \quad (n = 1, 2, 3, \dots) $$ The fundamental frequency is $f_1 = v/(2L)$.

Standing Waves in Closed Pipes (Open at One End, Closed at the Other)

This model applies to wind instruments like clarinets (approximately) and some organ pipes. At the open end, an antinode forms. At the closed end, air particles cannot move, so a node (zero displacement/pressure variation maximum) must form.

  • The simplest standing wave ($n=1$) has a node at the closed end and an antinode at the open end. This represents one-quarter of a wavelength. Its wavelength is $4L$.
  • Only odd harmonics are produced in closed pipes.
  • Possible Wavelengths ($\lambda_n$): $$ L = n \frac{\lambda_n}{4} \implies \lambda_n = \frac{4L}{n} \quad (n = 1, 3, 5, \dots) $$
  • Resonant Frequencies ($f_n$): Using $f = v/\lambda$: $$ f_n = \frac{v}{\lambda_n} = \frac{nv}{4L} \quad (n = 1, 3, 5, \dots) $$ The fundamental frequency is $f_1 = v/(4L)$.

Understanding standing waves is critical for acoustical engineering, the design of musical instruments, and the study of wave behavior in confined spaces. The specific wavelengths and frequencies generated determine the timbre and pitch of musical notes.
